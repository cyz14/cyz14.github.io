    <h1 id="auto-1">1<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Differential Forms<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h1>
    <p>
      微分形式是流形上实值函数的推广。一个微分
      \(k\)-形式给每个点分配一个切空间上的
      \(k\)-余向量。
    </p>
    <p>
      首先，微分形式是任意流形上内蕴的对象，所以可以用来构造流形的微分同胚不变量。不同于向量场，虽然也是内蕴的，微分形式有更丰富的代数结构。由于外积，分级和外导数的存在，光滑形式的集合同时是分级代数和微分复形。Moreover,
      the differential complex of smooth forms on a manifold can be pulled
      back under a smooth map, making the complex into a contravariant functor
      called the de Rham complex of the manifold. We will eventually construct
      the de Rham cohomology of a mnifold from the de Rham complex.
    </p>
    <p>
      Because integration of functions on a Euclidean space depends on a
      choice of coordinates and is not invariant under a change of
      coordinates, it is not possible to integrate functions on a manifold. 
    </p>
    <p>
      Among differentail forms, those of top degree turn out to transform
      correctly under a change of coordinats and are precisely the objects
      that can be integrated. The theory of integration on a manifold would
      not be possible without differential forms. 
    </p>
    <p>
      Very loosely speaking, differentail forms are whatever appears under an
      integral sign. In this sense, differential forms are as old as calculus,
      and many theorems in calculus such as Cauthy's integral theorem or
      Green's theorem can be interpreted as statements about differential
      forms. Cartan defined formally the algebra of differential forms on
      \(\mathbb{R}^n\) as the anticommutative graded algebra over
      \(C^{\infty}\) functions generated by \(d x^1, \ldots, d x^n\) in degree
      1. In the same paper one finds for the first time the exteriror
      derivative on differential forms. The modern definition of a
      differential form as a section of an exterior power of the cotangent
      bundle appeared in the late fourties, after the theory of fiber bundle
      came into being.
    </p>
    <p>
      In this chapter we give an intro to differential forms from the vector
      bundle point of view. For simplicity we start with 1-forms, which
      already have many of the properties of \(k\)-forms. We give various
      characterizations of smooth forms, and show how to multiply,
      differentiate, and pull back these forms. In addition to the exterior
      derivative, we also introduce the Lie derivative and interior
      multiplication, two other intrinsic operations on a manifold.
    </p>
    <h2 id="auto-2">1<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Differential 1-Forms<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h2>
    <p>
      Let \(M\) be a smooth manifold and \(p\) a point in \(M\). The cotagent
      space of \(M\) at \(p\), denoted by  \(T^{\ast}_p (M)\) or \(T^{\ast}_p
      M\), is defined to be the dual space of the tangent space \(T_p M\):
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle T_p^{\ast} M = (T_p M)^{\vee} =\operatorname{Hom} (T_p
      M, \mathbb{R}) .\)
    </center>
    <p>
      An element of the cotangent space is called a covector at \(p\). Thus, a
      covector \(\omega_p\) is a linear function
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle \omega_p : T_p M \rightarrow \mathbb{R}.\)
    </center>
    <p>
      A covector field, a differential 1-form, or more simply a 1-form on
      \(M\), is a function \(\omega\) that assigns to each point a covector
      \(\omega_p\) at \(p\). There are many reasons for the great utility of
      diff forms in manifold theory, among which is the fact that they can be
      pulled back under a map. This is in contract to vector fields, which in
      general cannot be pushed forward under a map.
    </p>
    <p>
      Covector fields arise naturally even when one is interested only in
      vector fields. For example, if \(X\) is a \(C^{\infty}\) vector field on
      \(\mathbb{R}^n\), then at each point \(p \in \mathbb{R}^n\), \(X_p =
      \sum a^i \partial / \partial x^i |_p\). The coefficient \(a^i\) depends
      on the vector \(X_p\). It is in fact a linear function: \(T_p
      \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), i.e., a covector at \(p\). As
      \(p\) varies over \(\mathbb{R}^n\), \(a^i\) becomes a covector field on
      \(\mathbb{R}^n\). Indeed, it is none other than the 1-form \(d x^i\)
      that picks out the \(i\)th coefficient of a vector field relative to the
      standard frame \(\partial / \partial x^1, \ldots, \partial / \partial
      x^n\).
    </p>
    <h3 id="auto-3">1.1<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>The Differential of a Function<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h3>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em">
      <strong>定义 <class style="font-style: normal">1.1</class>. </strong><i>If \(f\)
      is a \(C^{\infty}\) real-valued function on a manifold \(M\), its
      differential is defined to be the 1-form \(d f\) on \(M\) such that for
      any \(p \in M\) and \(X_p \in T_p M\),</i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-bottom: 1em">
      <i><center>
        \(\displaystyle (d f)_p (X_p) = X_p f.\)
      </center></i>
    </p>
    <p>
      Instead of \((d f)_p\), we also write \(d f|_p\) for the value of the
      1-form \(d f\) at \(p\). This is parallel to the two notations for a
      tangent vector: \((d / d t)_p = d / d t|_p\).
    </p>
    <p>
      We encountered anothe notion of the differential, denoted by
      \(f_{\ast}\) for a map \(f\) between manifolds. Let us compare the two
      notions of the differential.
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.2</class>. </strong><i>If \(f :
      M \rightarrow N\) is a \(C^{\infty}\) function, then for \(p \in M\) and
      \(X_p \in T_p M\),</i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-bottom: 1em">
      <i><center>
        \(\displaystyle f_{\ast} (X_p) = (d f)_p (X_p) \frac{d}{d t} |_{f (p)}
        .\)
      </center></i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em">
      <strong>证明. </strong>\(f_{\ast} (X_p) \in T_{f (p)}
      \mathbb{R}\), there is a real number \(a\) such that
    </p>
    <table width="100%">
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" align="center">\(\displaystyle f_{\ast} (X_p) = a \frac{d}{d t}
        |_{f (p)} .\)</td>
        <td align="right">(1.1)</td>
      </tr>
    </table>
    <p>
      To evaluate \(a\), apply both sides to \(x\):
    </p>
    <p style="margin-bottom: 1em">
      <center>
        \(\displaystyle a = f_{\ast} (X_p) (t) = X_p (t \circ f) = X_p f = (d
        f)_p (X_p) .\)
      </center>
      <span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>
      \(\Box\)
    </p>
    <p>
      This proposition shows that under the canonical identification of the
      tangent space \(T_{f (p)} \mathbb{R}\) with \(\mathbb{R}\) via
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle a \frac{d}{d t} |_{f (p)} \leftrightarrow a,\)
    </center>
    <p>
      \(f_{\ast}\) is the same as \(d f\). For this reason, we are justified
      in calling both of them the differential of \(f\).
    </p>
    <h3 id="auto-4">1.2<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Local Expression for a Differential
    1-Form<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h3>
    <p>
      Let \((U, \phi) = (U, x^1, \ldots, x^n)\) be a coordinate chart on a
      manifold \(M\). Then the differentials \(d x^1, \ldots, d x^n\) are
      1-forms on \(U\).
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.3</class>. </strong><i>At each
      point \(p \in U\), the covectors \((d x^1)_p, \ldots, (d x^n)_p\) form a
      basis for the cotangent space \(T_p^{\ast} M\) dual to the basis
      \(\partial / \partial x^1 |_p, \ldots, \partial / \partial x^n |_p\) for
      the tangent spacce \(T_p M\).</i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>证明. </strong>
      <center>
        \(\displaystyle (d x^i) \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j} |_p
        \right) =
\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j} |_p x^i = \delta_j^i .\)
      </center>
      <span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>
      \(\Box\)
    </p>
    <p>
      Every 1-form \(\omega\) can be written as a linear combination
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle \omega = \sum a_i d x^i,\)
    </center>
    <p>
      where the coefficients \(a_i\) are functions on \(U\). 
    </p>
    <p>
      To find \(a_i\), we apply the usual trick of evaluating both sides on
      \(\partial / \partial x^j\):
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle (d f) \left( \frac{\partial}{\partial x^j} \right) =
      \sum_i a_i d x^i \left(
\frac{\partial}{\partial x^j} \right)
      \Rightarrow \frac{\partial f}{\partial
x^j} = \sum_i a_i \delta_j^i =
      a_j .\)
    </center>
    <p>
      This gives a local expression for \(d f\)
    </p>
    <table width="100%">
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" align="center">\(\displaystyle d f = \sum \frac{\partial
        f}{\partial x^i} d x^i .\)</td>
        <td align="right">(1.2)</td>
      </tr>
    </table>
    <h3 id="auto-5">1.3<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>The Cotangent Bundle<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h3>
    <table width="100%">
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" align="center">\(\displaystyle T^{\ast} M := \bigcup_{p \in M}
        T_p^{\ast} M.\)</td>
        <td align="right">(1.3)</td>
      </tr>
    </table>
    <p>
      Mimicking the construction of tangent bundle, we give \(T^{\ast} M\) a
      topology as follows. If \((U, \phi)\) is a chart on \(M\) and \(p \in
      M\), then each \(\alpha \in T_p^{\ast} M\) can be written uniquely as a
      linear combination
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle \alpha = \sum c_i (\alpha) d x^i |_p .\)
    </center>
    <p>
      This gives rise to a bijection
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle \begin{array}{rl}
  \tilde{\phi} : T^{\ast} U &
      \rightarrow \phi (U) \times \mathbb{R}^n,
  \hspace{3cm}  
      \text{(1.4)}\\
  \alpha & \mapsto (\phi (p), c_1 (\alpha), \ldots, c_n
      (\alpha)) = (\phi
  \circ \pi, c_1, \ldots, c_n) (\alpha)
      .
\end{array}\)
    </center>
    <p>
      Under this bijection, we can transfer the topology of \(\phi (U) \times
      \mathbb{R}^n\) to \(T^{\ast} M\).
    </p>
    <p>
      Properly speaking, the cotangent bundle of a manifold is the triple
      \((T^{\ast} M, M, \pi)\), while \(T^{\ast} M\) and \(M\) are the total
      space and the base space, but by abuse of language, it is customary to
      call \(T^{\ast} M\) the cotangent bundle of \(M\).
    </p>
    <p>
      In terms of the cotangent bundle, a 1-form on \(M\) is simply a section
      of the cotangent bundle \(T^{\ast} M\); i.e., it is a map \(\omega : M
      \rightarrow T^{\ast} M\) such that \(\pi \circ \omega = \mathbb{1}_M\),
      the identity map on \(M\). We say that a 1-form \(\omega\) is
      \(C^{\infty}\) if it is \(C^{\infty}\) as a map \(M \rightarrow T^{\ast}
      M\).
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em">
      <strong>例 <class style="font-style: normal">1.4</class>. </strong>(Liouville form on
      the cotangent bundle). If a manifold \(M\) has dimension \(n\), then the
      total space \(T^{\ast} M\) of its cotangent bundle \(\pi : T^{\ast} M
      \rightarrow M\) is a manifold of dimension \(2 n\). Remarkably, on
      \(T^{\ast} M\) there is a 1-form \(\lambda\), called the Liouville form
      (or the Poincare form in some books), defined independently of charts as
      follows. A point in \(T^{\ast} M\) is a covector \(\omega_p \in
      T_p^{\ast} M\). If \(X_{\omega_p}\) is a tangent vector to \(T^{\ast}
      M\) at \(\omega_p\), then the pushforward \(\pi_{\ast} (X_{\omega_p})\)
      is a tangent vector to \(M\) at \(p\). Therefore, one can pair up
      \(\omega_p\) and \(\pi_{\ast} (X_{\omega_p})\) to obtain a real number
      \(\omega_p (\pi_{\ast} (X_{\omega_p}))\). Define
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle \lambda_{\omega_p} (X_{\omega_p}) = \omega_p (\pi_{\ast}
      (X_{\omega_p})) .\)
    </center>
    <p style="margin-bottom: 1em">
      The cotangent bundle and the Liouville form on it play an important role
      in the mthematical theory of classical mechanics.
    </p>
    <h3 id="auto-6">1.4<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Characterization of \(C^{\infty}\)
    1-Form<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h3>
    <p>
      The set of all smooth 1-forms on \(M\) has the structure of a vector
      space, denoted by \(\Omega^1 (M)\). 
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>引理 <class style="font-style: normal">1.5</class>. </strong><i>Let
      \((U, \phi)\) be a chart on a manifold \(M\). A 1-form \(\omega = \sum
      a_i d x^i\) on \(U\) is smooth if and only if the coefficient functions
      \(a_i\) are all smooth.</i>
    </p>
    <p>
      
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.6</class>.
      </strong><i>(Smoothness of a 1-form in terms of coefficients). Let
      \(\omega\) be a 1-form on a manifold \(M\). The following are
      equivalent:</i>
    </p>
    <div style="margin-bottom: 1em">
      <i><ol>
        <li>
          <p>
            The 1-form \(\omega\) is smooth on \(M\).
          </p>
        </li>
        <li>
          <p>
            The manifold \(M\) has an atlas such that on any chart \((U, x^1,
            \ldots, x^n)\) of the atlas, the coefficients \(a_i\) of \(\omega
            = \sum a_i d x^i\) relative to the frame \(d x^i\) are all smooth.
          </p>
        </li>
        <li>
          <p>
            On any chart \((U, x^1, \ldots, x^n)\) on the manifold, the
            coefficients \(a_i\) of \(\omega = \sum a_i d x^i\) relative to
            the frame \(d x^i\) are all smooth.
          </p>
        </li>
      </ol></i>
    </div>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>推论 <class style="font-style: normal">1.7</class>. </strong><i>If \(f\)
      is a \(C^{\infty}\) function on a manifold \(M\), then its differential
      \(d f\) is a \(C^{\infty}\) 1-form on \(M\).</i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.8</class>.
      </strong><i>(Linearity of a 1-form over functions) Let \(\omega\) be a
      1-form on a manifold \(M\). If \(f\) is a function and \(X\) is a vector
      field on \(M\), then \(\omega (f X) = f \omega (X)\).</i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.9</class>.
      </strong><i>(Smoothness of a 1-form in terms of vector fields). A 1-form
      \(\omega\) on a manifold \(M\) is \(C^{\infty}\) if and only if for
      every \(C^{\infty}\) vector field \(X\) on \(M\), the function \(\omega
      (X)\) is \(C^{\infty}\) on M.</i>
    </p>
    <h3 id="auto-7">1.5<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Pullback of 1-Forms<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h3>
    <p>
      If \(F : N \rightarrow M\) is a \(C^{\infty}\) map of manifolds, then at
      each point \(p \in N\) the differential
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle F_{\ast, p} : T_p N \rightarrow T_{F (p)} M\)
    </center>
    <p>
      is a linear map that pushes forward vectors at \(p\) from \(N\) to
      \(M\). The codifferential, i.e., the dual of the differential 
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle (F_{\ast, p})^{\vee} : T_{F (p)}^{\ast} M \rightarrow
      T_p^{\ast} N,\)
    </center>
    <p>
      reverse the arrow and pulls back a covector at \(F (p)\) from \(M\) to
      \(N\). Another notation for the codifferential is \(F^{\ast} = (F_{\ast,
      p})^{\vee}\). By the definition of the dual, if \(\omega_{F (p)} \in
      T_{F (p)}^{\ast} M\) is a covector at \(F (p)\) and \(X_p \in T_p N\) is
      a tangent vector at \(p\), then 
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle F^{\ast} (\omega_{F (p)}) (X_p) = ((F_{\ast, p})^{\vee}
      \omega_{F (p)}) (X_p)
= \omega_{F (p)} (F_{\ast, p} X_p) .\)
    </center>
    <p>
      We call \(F^{\ast} (\omega_{F (p)})\) the pullback of the covector
      \(\omega_{F (p)}\) by \(F\).
    </p>
    <p>
      Unlike vector fields, every covector field can be pulled back by a
      \(C^{\infty}\) map. Recall that functions can also be pulled back: if
      \(F\) is a \(C^{\infty}\) map from \(N\) to \(M\) and \(g \in C^{\infty}
      (M)\), then \(F^{\ast} g = g \circ F \in C^{\infty} (N)\).
    </p>
    <p>
      This difference in the behavior of vector fields and forms under a map
      can be traced to a basic asymmetry in the concept of a function &ndash;
      every point in the domain maps to only one image point in the range, but
      a point in the range can have several preimage points in the domain.
    </p>
    <p>
      A question naturally suggests itself. Is the pullback of a
      \(C^{\infty}\) 1-form under a \(C^{\infty}\) map \(C^{\infty}\)? To
      answer this question, we first need to establish three commutation
      properties of the pullback: its commutation with the differential, sum
      and product.
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.10</class>.
      </strong><i>(Commutation of the pullback with the differential) Let \(F
      : N \rightarrow M\) be a \(C^{\infty}\) map of manifolds. For any \(h
      \in C^{\infty} (M)\), \(F^{\ast} (d h) = d (F^{\ast} h)\).</i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em">
      <strong>证明. </strong>It suffices to check that for any
      point \(p \in N\) and any tangent vector \(X_p \in T_p N\),
    </p>
    <table width="100%">
      <tr>
        <td width="100%" align="center">\(\displaystyle (F^{\ast} d h)_p (X_p) = (d F^{\ast}
        h)_p (X_p) .\)</td>
        <td align="right">(1.5)</td>
      </tr>
    </table>
    <p style="margin-bottom: 1em">
      <span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>\(\Box\)
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.11</class>.
      </strong><i>(Pullback of a sum and a product). Let \(F : N \rightarrow
      M\) be a \(C^{\infty}\) map of manifolds. Suppose \(\omega, \tau \in
      \Omega^1 (M)\) and \(g \in C^{\infty} (M)\). Then</i>
    </p>
    <div style="margin-bottom: 1em">
      <i><ol>
        <li>
          <p>
            \(F^{\ast} (\omega + \tau) = F^{\ast} \omega + F^{\ast} \tau\),
          </p>
        </li>
        <li>
          <p>
            \(F^{\ast} (g \omega) = (F^{\ast} g) (F^{\ast} \omega)\).
          </p>
        </li>
      </ol></i>
    </div>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.12</class>.
      </strong><i>(Pullback of a \(C^{\infty}\) 1-form). The pullback
      \(F^{\ast} \omega\) of a \(C^{\infty}\) 1-form \(\omega\) on \(M\) under
      a \(C^{\infty}\) map \(F : N \rightarrow M\) is a \(C^{\infty}\) 1-form
      on N.</i>
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>例 <class style="font-style: normal">1.13</class>. </strong>(Liouville form on
      the cotangent bundle). Let \(M\) be a manifold. In terms of the
      pullback, the Liouville form \(\lambda\) on the cotangent bundle
      \(T^{\ast} M\) introduced in example 4 can be expressed as
      \(\lambda_{\omega_p} = \pi^{\ast} (\omega_p)\) at any \(\omega_p \in
      T^{\ast} M\).
    </p>
    <h3 id="auto-8">1.6<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Restrictions of 1-Forms to an Immersed
    Submanifold<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h3>
    <p>
      Let \(S \subset M\) be an immersed submanifold and \(i : S \rightarrow
      M\) the inclusion map. At any \(p \in M\), the differential \(i_{\ast} :
      T_p S \rightarrow T_p M\) is injective, one may view the tangent space
      \(T_p S\) as a subspace of \(T_p M\). If \(\omega\) is a 1-form on
      \(M\), then the restriction of \(\omega\) to \(S\) is the 1-form
      \(\omega |_S\) defined by
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle (\omega |_S)_p (v) = \omega_p (v) \quad
      \operatorname{for}\operatorname{all}p
\in S\operatorname{and}v \in T_p
      S.\)
    </center>
    <p>
      The following proposition shows that the restriction of 1-forms is
      simply the pullback of the inclusion \(i\).
    </p>
    <p style="margin-top: 1em; margin-bottom: 1em">
      <strong>命题 <class style="font-style: normal">1.14</class>. </strong><i>If \(i
      : S \hookrightarrow M\) is the inclusion map of an immersed submanifold
      \(S\) and \(\omega\) is a 1-form on \(M\), then \(i^{\ast} \omega =
      \omega |_S\).</i>
    </p>
    <h2 id="auto-9">2<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Differential k-Forms<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h2>
    <p>
      We now generalize the construction of 1-form on a manifold to
      \(k\)-forms. We show that locally they look no different from
      \(k\)-forms on \(\mathbb{R}^n\). We consturct the \(k\)th exterior power
      \(\bigwedge^k (T^{\ast} M)\) of the cotangent bundle. A differential
      \(k\)-form is seen to be a section of the bundle \(\bigwedge^k (T^{\ast}
      M)\). This gives a natural notion of smoothness as a section of the
      vector bundle \(\bigwedge^k (T^{\ast} M)\). The pullback and the wedge
      product of differential forms are defined pointwise. As examples of
      differential forms, we consider left-invariant forms on a Lie group.
    </p>
    <h3 id="auto-10">2.1<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span>Differential Forms<span style="margin-left: 1em"></span></h3>
    <p>
      \(k\)-tensor
    </p>
    <center>
      \(\displaystyle f : V \times \cdots \times V \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\)
    </center>
    <p>
      \(f\) is alternating if for any permutation \(\sigma \in S_k\)
    </p>
    <table width="100%">
      <tr>
